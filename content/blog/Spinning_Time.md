# Spinning Time

Timelines can be useful tools for visualizing how historical events unfolded. The JS Timeline tool from [Knightlab](http://timeline.knightlab.com/?_gl=1*1tws194*_ga*MTQ0MDQzMzkwNC4xNzMxNjIyNTcw*_ga_8F4WPDMPL5*MTczMjEyOTM1NC4yLjEuMTczMjEyOTM3MS4wLjAuMA..) allows the user to create digital timelines and publish them on the web. On this post I record a few thoughts regarding creating a digital timeline with the JS Timeline tool.

I chose to create a timeline about residential segregation ordinances in twentieth century America. I am addressing this topic in my thesis and already have sources complied. JS Timeline creators suggest limiting your timeline to twenty events or less. My current published timeline has ten events, but I may add more later. 

To get started, I copied the spreadsheet that JS Timeline provided and opened my copy in Google docs. The spreadsheet is already tabulated to insert data into the timeline, all I have to do is plug my data into the appropriate row and column, click publish to the web, and copy/paste the link to the spreadsheet into the timeline generator. After this is done, I can embed my timeline into a website, either via a URL or shortcode.

![Timeline image 2](https://doraimpson.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/tempImageuInWH2.heic)

The process sounds easy. I will say that, for the most part, it was easy, except for occasional glitches that occur. For instance, I had a difficult time inserting images into my timeline. Any image or multimedia must have a URL associated with it in order to be inserted into the Timeline spreadsheet. Photos that I have taken myself gave me the most difficulty since they were not from a website. To work around this issue, I uploaded these images to my website in a post and then inserted that URL into the timeline spreadsheet. There is most likely a better way to deal with this issue, but for now, it works.

I find timelines to be valuable digital tools in that they offer a quick way to communicate the basics about a historical event in a manner that can be easily understood. In a sense, they are a very "public-facing" tool. Timelines are known for brevity; too many words will visually bog them down. If you want an audience to have quick exposure to concepts or events, including how those events unfolded, then a timeline might be one of the best visual tools that can be used.
